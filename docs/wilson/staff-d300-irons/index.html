About the Wilson Staff D300

The D300 was Wilson's distance game-improvement iron, announced on 10 January 2017 and on sale from 23 January. Its FLX Face technology used Power Holes filled with TE031 urethane running all the way around the perimeter of the face, which Wilson said left about three quarters of the face free to flex, and weight pods machined from 17-4 stainless steel sat in the heel and toe of a wide, relieved sole. Lofts step in a widening progression from a 20.5-degree 4-iron to a 43-degree pitching wedge, with a 29.5-degree 7-iron, about a degree stronger than the D200 through the middle irons. The stock set is 4-iron through pitching wedge with a 48-degree gap wedge, and a 54-degree sand wedge was sold to match. Figures below are the standard men's steel-shaft specification.

Frequently asked questions

What is the loft of a Wilson Staff D300 7-iron?

The Wilson Staff D300 7-iron has a loft of 29.5 degrees, with a 62-degree lie and a 37.5-inch steel-shaft length.

What clubs come in a Wilson Staff D300 set?

The stock set is 4-iron through pitching wedge plus a 48-degree gap wedge. A 54-degree sand wedge was sold to match.

What are Power Holes on the Wilson Staff D300?

They are slots around the perimeter of the face, filled with TE031 urethane, that let most of the face flex independently of the body. Wilson called the system FLX Face.

How do the D300 lofts compare with the D7 that replaced it?

The D7 is roughly a degree stronger through the middle of the set. The D7 7-iron is 28 degrees against the D300's 29.5, and the pitching wedge is 43 degrees in both.

Finding a used set

The Wilson Staff D300 ran from 2017 to 2019 and is no longer in production, so the used market is the only source. Search eBay, 2nd Swing, Golf Avenue and the PGA Tour Superstore trade-in listings, and check the loft and lie against the chart above before buying — sets this age have often been bent from standard.
