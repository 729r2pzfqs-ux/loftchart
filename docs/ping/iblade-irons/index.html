About the Ping iBlade

The iBlade was Ping's tour-players iron between the end of the S series and the arrival of the Blueprint, a compact cast 431 stainless head with a thin top rail, a straight leading edge, a high toe and nearly pure tungsten weights pressed into every toe to raise MOI. Lofts sit a degree weaker than the i200 of the following year — the 7-iron is 34 degrees — with offset of 0.09 inches at that club and added bounce and a heel grind for turf interaction. It runs 3-iron through pitching wedge. Ping's colour-code (dot) system sets lie angle relative to standard: gold 4 flat, brown 3 flat, orange 2 flat, red 1 flat, BLACK standard, blue 1 upright, green 2 upright, white 3 upright, silver 4 upright, maroon 5 upright. Frequently asked questions

What is the loft of a Ping iBlade 7-iron?

The Ping iBlade 7-iron has a loft of 34 degrees, a standard (black dot) lie of 62.0 degrees and a steel-shaft length of 37.0 inches.

When were Ping iBlade irons made?

The iBlade was introduced in 2016 and was succeeded by the Blueprint in 2019.

Is the Ping iBlade a blade?

No, despite the name. It is a compact cast 431 stainless cavity back with tungsten toe weighting. Ping's actual muscle-back blade is the Blueprint, which followed it and has roughly half the iBlade's offset at the 7-iron — 0.05 inches against 0.09.

What is the difference between the Ping iBlade and the i200?

The i200 is a degree stronger through the long and mid irons — its 3-iron is 19 degrees against the iBlade's 20 — carries noticeably more offset in the long irons, and adds a utility wedge to the set.

Finding a used set

The Ping iBlade ran from 2016 to 2019 and is no longer in production, so the used market is the only source. Search eBay, 2nd Swing, Golf Avenue and the PGA Tour Superstore trade-in listings, and check the loft and lie against the chart above before buying — sets this age have often been bent from standard.
